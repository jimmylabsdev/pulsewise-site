Why PulseWise exists

A note from the person who built it

I have hypertension. Not the kind you manage with a vague resolution to eat better — the kind that, at its peak, once read 280 over 260 on the cuff. I remember the reading because I had to read it twice. The monitor wasn't broken. I was.

I also have hypertensive retinopathy. The blood pressure damaged the small vessels in my retina, and now my eyes are part of the long list of things I have to watch carefully. There are doctor visits — for the BP, for the eyes, sometimes both in the same week. There are medications, dosages, timings. There is a cuff on my desk and another in my bag. And there is the same routine, every single day: a reading in the morning, a reading in the evening, written down somewhere I can find again.

It changed how I think about every day. Not in a dramatic way — more like the way a stone in your shoe changes how you walk. You stop noticing it consciously, but it shapes everything anyway. Mornings start with a number. Evenings end with one. Decisions about coffee, sleep, salt, stress, exercise — they all run through the filter of "what is this going to do to my reading tonight?"

So I went looking for an app. I assumed this was a solved problem. It is not.

What I found

The paid apps wanted subscriptions. Eight dollars a month, twelve dollars a month, sometimes more, to write down a number that takes me four seconds to record on a piece of paper. They dressed it up with charts and "insights" and gamification, but the core transaction was the same: pay us monthly to store your own health data on our servers.

The free apps were worse. Pop-up ads between readings. Banner ads under the chart. Full-screen interstitials when I opened the app at six in the morning to log a measurement before my first cup of water. One of them played a video ad with sound. At six a.m. While I was sitting in the dark trying to be quiet so I wouldn't wake anyone up. I deleted it before the ad finished.

None of them felt like a place I wanted to spend two minutes a day with my own health. They felt like marketing funnels with a BP logger bolted on. And the worst part is that I understand why — health apps have to make money somehow, and ads and subscriptions are how most apps make money. I don't blame the developers. I just couldn't use what they had built.

What I wanted instead

A notebook. That's all. A quiet, private place to write down a number twice a day, see how it's changing over time, and hand the whole thing to my doctor every few months. No accounts, because I don't want my BP history attached to an email address that could be breached. No cloud sync, because the data should live on my phone, not on someone's server. No ads, ever, because the moment a health app shows you an ad it has stopped being a health app and started being something else.

And one specific thing I kept wishing for: voice entry. Because half the time I'm taking my BP one-handed — the cuff is on my left arm, the monitor is on the desk, and my right hand is holding the cuff in place or steadying the tube. Typing is awkward. Speaking is not. "One twenty over eighty, pulse seventy-two" is faster than tapping, and it's the way I'd say it to my doctor anyway.

What PulseWise is

PulseWise is the app I wanted and couldn't find. You speak your reading. The numbers get extracted and confirmed. You save it. That's it. The history is on your phone. The trends are clear. When you need to share with a doctor, one tap gives you a clean PDF or CSV of everything.

There is no account. There is no cloud. There are no ads. There is no subscription. There is no upsell screen, no "premium" tier, no trial. The whole app is free, and it stays free, and the only thing it ever asks of you is the reading you wanted to log in the first place.

Voice transcripts, when they need to go off your device for parsing, are processed in memory and discarded immediately. Nothing is stored on a server. There is no server, in any meaningful sense. There is just the app, on your phone, doing one thing carefully.

What PulseWise is not

It is not a medical device. It does not measure your blood pressure — your monitor does that. It does not diagnose anything. It does not tell you what to do about your readings, because that is your doctor's job, and your doctor knows your full history in a way no app ever will. The categories shown in the app (Low, Normal, High) are simplified thresholds for quick orientation, not clinical guidelines.

If you are in a medical emergency, do not open this app. Call your local emergency services. PulseWise is for the long, slow work of tracking — not for moments when something is acutely wrong.

If this is useful to you

If you're reading this because you also live with hypertension, or you're tracking BP for a parent or a partner, or you just got a diagnosis and you're trying to figure out how to incorporate one more thing into your day — I hope PulseWise makes that easier. It was built for people like us, by someone like us. There is no team, no investors, no growth targets. There is one person with a cuff and a problem and enough engineering to do something about it.

If something in the app doesn't work, or if there's a feature you wish it had, write to me. I read every email. pulsewise@vigshrav.com.

Take care of yourself. Watch your numbers. See your doctor.

— The person behind PulseWise
